CHAPTER 7

REGULATORY AND LEGAL ASPECTS OF HEALTH INSURANCE

Chapter Introduction

Insurance is promise sold to the customer. In order to maintain the trust of people, it is imperative that the insurer honours the promise. A variety of risks such as insurance risk, invesrment risk or liquidity risk endanger the fullfillment of this promise. Self-regulation can protect the insurer against these risks. However an independent regulatory body is needed to add an extra degree of control on the insurance business. Also the regulatory authority needs to have the adequate amount of legal backing in order to do its job to the best of ability. The chapter discusses the need for health insurance regulation, the principles of health regulation and various measures taken to enforce regulation. We also look at health insurance regulation in the Indian context and the role played by the Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority (IRDA).

Learning Outcomes

A. Principles and practice of health insurance regulation B. Health insurance regulation in the Indian context

Look at this Scenario

Would you buy / renew health insurance if you noticed the following things? 9 Renewal sometimes rejected on flimsy grounds 9 Claim settlement takes a long time 9 Premiums are hiked in an arbitary manner The obvious answer would be "NO". That leads us to the next question. How do we prevent the above situations from happening? The answer lies in regulation. Indian health insurance market is booming currently and has a lot of issues that need to be taken care of. The notion of leaving regulation to the “good sense” of the companies does not always work. Robust regulations are needed to “clean up” the grey areas in health insurance and ensure a greater transparency.

A. Principles and practice of health insurance regulation

  1. Need for health insurance regulation

As discussed earlier in Chapter 1, the health insurance market has several imperfections: 9 9 9 9

Information asymmetry operating at both (insurer’s and insured’s) end Risk selection by insurers Adverse selection of insured Moral hazard present at the demand side (insured) or supply side (health providers)

Regulatory intervention is needed to ensure that the health insurance market is not adversely affected by these market imperfections. A health insurance company manages the insurance risk transferred to it through the insurance contract. However this is not the only uncertainty managed by it. There are various other types of risks which a health insurer is required to manage in addition. To ensure that the insurers manage such risks effectively regulatory oversight is required.

2. Health insurance provider – Types of risk

a) Insurance risk

i. Underwriting practices and risk appetite of the insurer give rise to this type of risk. ii. In health insurance, each risk by itself is not very large but there still exists the risk of ‘concentration’. iii. Reckless underwriting can create trouble for the insurer. iv. Reinsurance mitigates this risk, and is discussed in more details in Chapter 10.

b) Investment risk

i. The occurrence of this type of risk depends on the mix of investment options chosen by the insurer, as also the overall environment in the economy. ii. Even if the insurer holds good quality assets, holding long term debt can lead to liquidity risk. iii. Complex investment products may be very risky (as can be seen from what happened with AIG in 2008).

c) Credit risk

i. As in any other business with financial dealings, there is risk of default by business associates like providers, suppliers and reinsurers.

d) Liquidity risk

i. Even if the insurer is profitable, there can still be a mismatch between the cash flows, and current assets and liabilities. ii. This mismatch reduces the insurer’s ability to arrange adequate cash to pay its liabilities at a short notice.

e) Business environment risk

i. The insurer may be exposed to changes in the policy environment and the economic environment which may affect it adversely ii. Operational and other risks including risk of frauds, risk of not keeping up with technology, risk of losing key human resources etc. These risks are not discrete and often overlap and interplay. This requires the top management to actively manage these risks, along with regulatory oversight. While self-regulation has been stated as an option, the incentives to manage risks are different from a regulator’s perspective, thus an independent regulator is more effective.

3. Institutional failures and regulations

Institutional failures are, in general, caused by lax management, weak corporate governance, poor central controls and supervision, unsound accounting systems and other infrastructure compounded by weaknesses in the legal framework (IAIS, 1997). A study of 21 companies in financial trouble found that management issues were at the root of the problem (Lorent, 2008 citing Ashby, McDonnell and Sharma, 2002). Moreover, the complexity of health insurance business, the multiple risks which health insurers manage, and the market failures in the sector, all require a very effective top management role. When the regulator mandates a particular level of corporate governance, the level of regulatory involvement can decrease. Mechanisms like independent directors, actuaries, internal auditors etc. are often required (but that alone too did not work in the case of AIG). Thus, the complexity of health insurance business makes it very vulnerable to the effects of poor corporate governance. Regulation monitors and brings about timely corrective changes to reduce the risk of institutional failure. Regulation also serves to harmonise the activities of different players in order to achieve the desired social objectives from health insurance most notable of which is equitable access to health. . Health insurance is ultimately a promise, and regulation is the key to maintaining customer’s confidence in the ‘promise’ of health insurance.

  1. Why a failed health insurer has major repercussions?

A health insurer sells a ‘promise’ to honour a valid claim, and the consumer’s trust in the insurer keeping its promise is paramount. If an insurer fails, people would lose their confidence in the entire health insurance system. Also, in health insurance, the policyholder has long-term expectations for continued coverage. It is always more difficult to switch insurers as this may entail loss of benefits. Policyholders who are already ill and in need of ongoing medical care may lose coverage because of the insurer’s failure. New insurers can decline those who present a higher risk of making claims or exclude certain conditions and give them poorer coverage than they already had. Finally, even otherwise, health insurance coverage becomes more comprehensive due to continuous renewal of insurance, completion of waiting periods related to the 1-year, 2-year and 4-year exclusions which stand covered. If a person switches insurers, he may then have to undergo these waiting periods all over again.

a) Regulatory structure

i. Statutory base

The following points are an important statutory base for the regulator. 9 Legal sanctity for the regulatory function 9 An independent source of financing 9 Adequate ’teeth’ for the regulator to take penal action

ii. Administrative location

A health insurance regulator can be housed within a Health ministry or in an Insurance regulatory agency. In the context of a small country with limited regulatory manpower, or in the case of regulator dealing with integrated insurers which also underwrite non-health businesses, having a health insurance regulator within the overall insurance regulatory structure is an option. In the case of several countries, an independent Health Insurance Agency located outside the health ministry and also outside the regulatory mechanism for non-health insurance entities is also an option.

iii. Licensing and registration

These are the regulator’s tools to validate the management background, their integrity, financial strength and business plans before granting the insurance licence to any entity. These aspects can be important in ensuring the safety of large amounts of public money which the insurers will be managing once they have a licence.

iv. Financial regulation

Regulators usually lay down prudential accounting regulations and rules, financial reporting requirements, and undertake constant monitoring of the financial position of the insurance companies to ensure that they stay solvent and their accounting statements are true and fair.

v. Product regulation

Regulators lay down filing or approval requirements for health insurance products. This includes transparency and fairness requirements to take care of the information asymmetry from the insurer’s side.

vi. Price regulation

In certain situations, regulator may also regulate the prices (and increases thereof) of health insurance products.

b) Enforcement of regulations

Ensuring enforcement is the essence of health insurance regulation. All regulations and monitoring systems could be meaningless without enforcement. Drafting and promulgation of regulations is not enough, and enforcement is the key to achieving regulatory objectives. Enforcement begins with careful off-site monitoring supplemented by on-site visits as necessary. Effective action for breaches or violations is the key to prevention of such breaches or violations by the same and other entities. This shows that the regulator ‘means business’. This also serves as a deterrent to similar breaches in the future. Regulatory actions for breaches or violations can include: 9 9 9 9 9

Restricting business activity or activities by the insurer(s) Levying fines and penalties on the insurer(s) Ordering removal/ change of personnel Issuing warnings to the insurer Appointing Liquidator/ Observer/ Administrator, etc.

c) Monitoring by the regulators

i. Offsite monitoring

This is done at the regulator’s office itself, without actually going to the offices of the insurance companies. It includes detailed examination of various documents like 9 9 9 9

MIS Reports, Accounts, Reinsurance contracts, Outsourcing arrangements etc.

IT systems can play a very important role in offsite monitoring as several of the processes can be automated and attention of the regulator can be immediately drawn if any unexpected or adverse information flows through the system.

ii. Onsite inspections

These are done to verify the reports submitted by the insurers, check their internal controls, and also examine the market conduct aspects of the insurers. These are a supplement and not a substitute of good off-site monitoring.

d) Regulation for consumer protection

Health insurance has several areas where regulators may need to ensure that the customer’s interests are protected. Some of the customer protection areas for regulatory intervention include

i. Access

Regulators may impose requirements like guaranteed issue or variants thereof, for some or all products in the market.

ii. Renewability and cancellation

Regulator may lay down certain provisions so that insurers cannot arbitrarily decline renewals or cancel policies, especially where they do so with the intention of ‘cream skimming’. The possible measures could include requirements for guaranteed renewability or for certain disclosures on renewability to be provided upfront to the consumers to enable them to make an informed decision.

iii. Portability across insurers, including creditable coverage from earlier insurers, may also be a regulatory requirement. iv. Product terms and conditions may also be regulated. There may be requirements like: 9 9 9 9

Standard products or minimum benefit coverage mandates Standardisation of definitions and terms Caps on waiting periods and cost-sharing provisions Requirements for fairness, transparency, disclosures

v. Prevention of miss-selling

Prescribing the correct qualifications, background and market conduct requirements for intermediaries is another area where regulators lay down the requirements and help prevent miss-selling and other market conduct issues.

vi. Grievance redressal

Often multiple channels exist for an aggrieved insurance consumer to take up his grievance with, which can include 9 Insurer’s internal review and grievance mechanism 9 Regulator’s review and monitoring mechanisms 9 External reviews or Ombudsman mechanisms 9 Consumer law, competition laws, company laws 9 Contract law and judicial mechanism Often, regulators take the onus to co-ordinate amongst these grievance mechanisms, to monitor the data flowing from grievances and also intervene where necessary. Grievance redressal is important for consumer’s confidence in insurance. The grievance channels also serve as ‘Eyes and Ears’ of the regulator to take action towards the causes of such grievances.

B. Health insurance regulation in the Indian context

Early years of legislative controls on the insurance sector were consolidated and updated as the Insurance Act of 1938, which, as revised from time to time. This does remain at the core of insurance laws for the country30. The regulatory structure, as also the constituents of the industry, underwent several changes over the next 7 decades, characterized particularly by two sets of events which shaped the course of the industry

  1. Nationalisation

Nationalisation of the Life Insurance industry in 1956 and the Non-life Insurance industry in 1973, wherein the large number of erstwhile private insurers were nationalised and merged into one Life Insurance Corporation and one General Insurance Corporation (the latter with four subsidiary companies) owned by the Government of India.

2. Reforms

The ‘Malhotra Committee’ (1994) submitted a report on reforms leading to the establishment of the Insurance Regulator – the interim regulator- Insurance Regulatory Authority (IRA) in 1996. The statutory authority, Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority (IRDA) was established in 2000. The Indian insurance industry is, thus, governed by Insurance Act, 1938, General Insurance Business (Nationalisation) Act, 1972, Life Insurance Corporation Act, 1956, and IRDA Act 1999.

  1. Role of IRDA

IRDA is not just a regulatory authority but also plays an active ‘development’ role in health insurance, which has been established in the preamble of IRDA Act, section 14 of IRDA Act and Section 3(2AA) of Insurance Act 1938. As health insurance in India continues to be in its early stages, the role of IRDA is also very crucial in preventing adverse consequences of the health insurance for cost of care, equity, consumer satisfaction, fraud and ethical standards. IRDA has stipulated several regulations for both life and non-life insurance companies in many aspects of business and the same are also applicable, where relevant, in case of health insurance. Let us look at the role of the Regulator in different aspects

a) Policyholder’s protection regulation and grievance redressal

In the General Insurance industry, the Regulator plays a vital role in the protection of interests of the stakeholders, including policyholders, insurers, insurance intermediaries etc. Insurance business being a contract of Uberrima Fidei or Utmost Good Faith, Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority (IRDA) facilitates transparency and fair business practices in the Indian Insurance industry through certain guidelines for both insurers and insured which aims to establish a healthy business environment. As part of its role as the apex body that governs the manner in which business is conducted in the insurance industry, IRDA has formed Policyholder Protection Regulations, 2002 with a view to safeguarding the interests of policyholders at large. The Policyholder Protection regulations, 2002 comprises a set of guidelines for insurers encompassing areas of point of sale, proposal form, grievance redressal, policy details, claims process and policy servicing.

These guidelines cover the entire policy lifecycle, allowing the insured to make an informed buying decision and avail of the policy benefits. The act mandates insurers to provide sufficient information to prospects regarding the aforementioned areas. The IRDA has a Grievance Redressal Cell which plays a facilitative role by taking up complaints against insurers with the respective companies for speedy resolution. The IRDA however does not adjudicate on complaints. As claims/policy contracts in dispute require adjudication and the IRDA does not carry out any adjudication, insured are advised to approach the available quasi-judicial or judicial channels, i.e., the Insurance Ombudsmen, Consumer fora or the Civil courts for such complaints.

b) Access to health insurance for senior citizens and rural/informal sector workers

IRDA act empowers it to issue regulations to the insurance company to cater to the needs of weaker and vulnerable section of population in specified manner. IRDA has laid down certain instructions for providing access to senior citizens in 2009. It has also prescribed obligations under rural & social sector which encourage insurers for making products specifically for health insurance under micro insurance regulations.

c) Role of IRDA in standardisation initiatives

IRDA has supported and actively participated in the pioneering initiatives through multi-stakeholder industry group, which worked together in addressing issues which concern health insurance. This has been discussed in an earlier chapter. In brief, these included Standard Treatment Guidelines (STGs) for 21 common causes of hospitalisation, standard definitions of critical illness enhancing the customer’s understanding of these terms and easier comparison of product offerings in the market, standardising the list of non-medical expenses smoothening the interaction between the patients, hospitals, TPAs, and insurers by minimizing ambiguities on what is payable under health insurance policies, standardization of important documents in health insurance system, like pre-authorization form and the claim form etc. The initiatives have culminated in guidelines on “Standardization in Health Insurance” issued in Feb 2013 by IRDA. Refer Annexure at the end of study material for detailed guidelines. These would help boost operational efficiency in the system as also yield data for industry’s development.

d) Pre Existing diseases (PEDs)

It is estimated that an overwhelming majority of claim disputes in non- life sector are related to medical (health) insurance and majority of these pertains to pre-existing diseases. This prompted the General Insurance Council to arrive at a standard definition for PEDs in 2008.

Definition

For all the medical expenses (indemnity) products Pre exiting diseases will be “Any condition, ailment or injury or related conditions(s) for which insured had signs or symptoms, and/or were diagnosed, and/ or received medical advice/ treatment, within 48 months prior to insured’s first policy with the insurer.”

PED exclusion wordings

“Benefits will not be available for any condition(s) as defined in the policy, until 48 months of continuous coverage have elapsed, since inception of the first policy with any insurer.” The essence of this definition is that it provides a maximum look back period of 48 months and a maximum waiting period of covering pre-existing diseases as 48 months. Insurance companies are at liberty to reduce these periods but not enhance it.

e) Renewability of health insurance policies

Regulator in consultation with the industry came up with landmark circulars on renewability of health insurance policies which has many provisions and lay emphasis on adequate disclosures, and promoting transparency and fair treatment for policyholders. These circulars helped in enhancing policyholder’s confidence in the health insurance system.

Summary

a) Regulatory interventions are needed to protect the health insurance market from the effects of imperfection like information asymmetry, adverse selection and moral hazard etc. b) The insurers have to manage different types of risks related to insurance contracts, liquidity, policy and market environment etc. c) Health insurance regulations are needed in order to maintain the trust of the people in health insurance. d) The regulatory structure comprises of the regulatory authority, licensing and registration mechanism, financial and product regulations along with price control. e) The regulator uses off-site and on-site monitoring for the purpose of regulating the insurer’s activity f) Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority (IRDA) has introduced several measures to protect the policyholder’s interest, address grievances and standardising the health insurance market
